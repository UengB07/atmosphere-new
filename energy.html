ENERGY

BASICS

There are many types of energy. Energy can be usually put into two categories, potential and kinetic. Potential is usually approximated as "stored" energy, though not quite and kinetic energy is energy in motion, though not quite. The general formula for potential energy (PE) is:

PE = mgh

(where m is the mass of the object, g is the gravitational constant, and h is the height from ground level).

while the general formula for kinetic energy (KE) is

KE = (1/2)mv2

(where m is the mass and v is the velocity).

Heat is usually an observation of kinetic energy, and in this subject, we see radiant energy as a form of kinetic energy of high importance.

Energy may not "created" or "destroyed." It can be translated and transformed.

Temperature

Temperature is a measure of the average speed of molecules in motion. We do not usually use m/s as our unit, we use an easier system based on Kelvin, Celsius or Farenheit.

Throughout this doucment, we will refer to warm air as less dense as the molecules, on average are farther apart and colder air as more dense.

Let us say you had two ponds, A and B. They both are the same temperature and contain the same internal energy.

Pour A into B. Now B has more internal energy than A even though they are same temperature. B is now more massive. The average speed of the molecules are stil the same in both.

You sip a cup of hot tea on a massive cool lake. The lake even though it is cooler has more internal energy but lower temperature and average speed of molecules. It is more massive.

Heat - a process of energy transfer.

There are many scales of temperature:

Gabriel Farhenheit Scale (°F)
Between boiling and freezing points of water at regular ground level pressure is divided into 180 units. Water here freezes at 32°F and boils at 212°F.
Anders Celsius Scale (°F)
Between boiling and freezing points of water at regular ground level pressure is divided into 100 units. Water here freezes at 0°C and boils at 100°C.
Lord Kelvin Scale (K)
Usually used for scientific purposes, and is 0°C is 273 K. Numbers may not be negative.

Conversions are as follows:

°F = (1.8)(°C) + 32

K = °C + 273

Some Heat Terms

Heat Capacity
the heat needed for a substance’s temperature to change by one degree, in units of Energy / Degree. Default: J / K.
Specific Heat
the amount of energy that must be added to one unit of mass of the substance to cause an increase of one degree Default: J / (kg * K).
Specific Heat Capacity
Same exact thing as specific heat.
Calorie
amount of heat required to raise the temperature of 1 g of water from 14.5°C to 15.5°C.

Phase Changes and Consequences

There are phase changes, such as liquid water turning into vapor or vapor turning into liquid, or even directly turning into solid. Let us say water is the system. In an endothermic phase change, water needs energy, or heat, which sometimes is called latent heat in certain situations, to change into a higher temperature phase. In an exothermic phase change, Energy is released from that water when the water can translated into a colder temperature phase.

Below are examples.

  • Evaporation - liquid to gas - endothermic
  • Sublimation - solid to gas - endothermic
  • Melting - solid to liquid - endothermic
  • Condensation - gas to liquid - exothermic
  • Freezing - liquid to solid - exothermic
  • Deposition - gas to solid - exothermic

The sun's energy evaporates water from the surface of the Earth. But when this vapor travels upward, it becomes too cold and the water molecules lose their speed and begin to become attracted to each other more significantly. Then condensation of water - lots of molecules - occurs. And from the above list, we see condensation is an exothermic reaction, so heat is released. This is of vital significance for energy to be translated into the effects of storms.

Three Forms of Energy Transfer

Energy can be trasnferred in a variety of ways. We will go over three ways.

Conduction
A molecule's energy and vibrations comes into contact with another. That molecule then absorbs some of that energy. Note that different molecules and aggregates of molecules have different abilities to do this, we can measure this by heat conductivities. Water is a poor conductor and air even poorer.

Radiation

Radiation consists of things called photons, which are quantized packets of energy. Packets of photons make up waves, and these waves are a form of radiation.

All things emit radiation.

Now the wavelengths of this radiation depend primarily on temperature.

Many of the objects you see are do not radiate in the wavelengths of human vision. So how we can see them? Because other sources of light, especially the Sun, provide waves to reflect and bounce off everywhere. (If there where none of this, everything would be dark).

The maximum rate of radiation by a square meter of an object is given in Watts per meter squared. This can be calculated through an equation using the Josef Stefan-Ludwig Boltzmann Constant: σ, which is 5.67 x 10-8 W/m2K4.

EA = σT4

Wilhelm Wein has another law:

λmax = ( 2900 μm x K ) / T

λmax is the wavelength of maximum emission, T is Temperature.

Objects absorb and emit radiation. An object that is a perfect absorber (it will absorb ALL the radiation that hits it) and a perfect emitter (it will do a perfect job on emitting the maximum radiation at a certain temperature) is called a blackbody.

A blackbody is theoretical, but things extremely close with little offset that match this definition are the Sun and the Earth.

Wein's Law and Stefan Boltzmann's Law usually apply to blackbodies.

Radiative Equilibirum - absorption of radiation equals the rate of emission.

There is a radiative equilibrium on Earth, it absorbs from Sun and emits out. The average temperature at which this occurs is called the radiative equi. temperature.

Earth's equilibirum temperature is -18°C. But we must make a significant addend to this. This is the Earth's atmosphere. Without the atmosphere, we would feel much colder.

My Greenhouse Gases, My Saviours and My Enemies

Kirchoff's Law of Radiation - for gases, good absorbers are good emitters at a particular wavelength, and poor absorbers are poor emitters at a wavelength.

Some gases in the atmosphere, called greenhouse gases, area able to capture the emitting radiation that was supposed to go to space, then emitting it back to the Earth. While all this is happening dynamically, on average, not all the Earth's emission escapes. This phenomenon is known as the atmopsheric greenhouse effects (note: the effect of warming in a greenhouse is caused for different reasons).

(There is a special exception, from 8 to 11 micrometers roughly, water vapor and carbon dioxide for some reason do not absorb this wavelength and so that Earth radiation is able to escape into space. This range is known as the atmospheric window).

Certain clouds can enhance the atm. greenhouse effect - the liquid cloud droplets in them also capture emitting radiation and emit it back. They even can capture the radiation in the range of the atmopsheric window. But some clouds (low thick) can also be significant emitters which radiate back to space.

In the last century, the average surface air temperature rose a degree Celsius. Carbon dioxide has been increasingly released into the atmosphere, leading to some warming and higher rates of evaporation of water. With more and more water molecules entering the gas phase rather than retaining their liquid life, they are able to increase IR radiation back to Earth.

Clouds and oceans are being studied, they could be a factor.
