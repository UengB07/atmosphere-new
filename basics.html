ATMOSPHERIC SCIENCE

BASICS

The atmosphere give us sound and beauty. Without it, it would be too hot or too cold. Even then, it has been more than 122°F in Death Valley once and lower than -121°F in Antarctica.

Earth's atmosphere is thin. It has a mass of 6 x 1024 kg.

The Early Atmosphere

The atmosphere formed 4.6 billion years ago. The first two gases were probably hydrogen and helium. Some molecules such as ammonia and methane might have existed.

Gases then formed out from volcanoes and steam vents (Earth was very hot back then). 80% of it was water vapor, and 10% carbon dioxide, then some nitrogen.

Millions of year passed while the constant outpouring of gases from the hot interior, known as outgassing created water vapor.

Thousands of years passed in which rain fell. Large amounts of carbon dioxide were dissolved into the formed oceans. With the water vapor and carbon dioxide decreasing, nitrogen soon took dominance of the atmosphere.

Finally, oxygen took dominance as the second most abundant gas by the splitting of water by photodisassociation and the generation of plants.

Vertical Structure

Air density and pressure decrease with increasing height. It can be approximated by the formula: (P0 = 1 atm at ground)

P = P0e[(-mgz)/KT]

Atmospheric Layer Appx Height Appx Temperature Appx Pressure
Tropopause 12 km -60°C 80-90 mb
Stratopause 48 km 0°C 1 mb
Mesopause 84 km -84°C below 0.01 mb

Beyond mesosphere is the thermosphere.

Troposphere: where the weather occurs. The tropshere can sometimes have an isothermal zone. The tropopause is usually higher over the equator, higher in summer and lower in wnter. Sometimes the tropopause breaks, in which the there is not a significant distiction between the troposhere and stratosphere, but rather, the air from both layers mix. Usually in this case you will set jet streams, which have high winds that can exceed 100 kts.

Stratosphere: usually a straified layer, there is reduced vertical motion here. It is inverted. A phenomenon known as sudden stratospheric warming occurs here. But that is not usually the case of why it gets warmer as altitude increases. Ozone absorbs UV light. Molecules are heated and excited by radiation (you will see "Chapman reactions" later). As density decreases, each ozone molecules gets more radiation and energy.

Mesosphere: The ozone excitement ends around the beginning of the mesosphere, as there are less ozone molecules, and burned radiation, hyopxia, and blood boiling can occur here.

Thermosphere: Molecules are highly radiated and get supercharged with energy. Energy of molecules increase tremendously. Although a molecule can travel over a km before hitting another, the radiation is so intense that it gets extremely hot. The heterosphere begins here, where heavier molecules are at the bottom and lighter at the top.

Meterology

Study of the weather. (Climate is the study of recorded and acculumated observations of weather over a long period of time). Includes air temperature, pressure, humidity precipitation, wind, and clouds and visibility.

Aristotle

340 B.C. - he wrote a book called Meteorologica based on meteoros, meaning "high from the sky". When he lived, anything that fell from the sky were considered meteors. We have changed the definition today and meteors must meet a certain criteria before truly being called so.

Throughout time, various inventions have been made to help understand the weather. After WWII, numerical models and computers helped predictions greatly.

Geostationary Satellite - this satellite travels at the same rate as Earth spins, which allows it to remain positioned above the same spot so it can continuously monitor what is taking place beneath it.

Wind Notation - If you are facing north and the wind is blowing in your face, the wind would be called a "north wind."
